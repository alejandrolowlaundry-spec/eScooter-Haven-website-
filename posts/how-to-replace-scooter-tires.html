How to Replace Electric Scooter Tires (Complete Guide)

March 5, 2025 11 min read Written by Jordan Kim

Key Takeaways

  • Most electric scooter tire replacements can be done at home in 45–90 minutes
  • The wheel removal process varies significantly by model — front wheels are usually simpler than rear
  • Using tire levers is essential; never use a flathead screwdriver (it punctures tubes)
  • Always check inside the tire for the object that caused the flat before installing a new tube
  • Inflate the new tube slightly before installation to prevent pinch flats

A flat tire is the most common repair any electric scooter rider will face. The good news: it's one of the most straightforward repairs you can do yourself, and you'll never have to wait for a repair shop again once you know how. This guide covers the full process for tube-type tires (the most common on budget and mid-range scooters) and tubeless tires.

Before You Start

Check your scooter's manual or the tire sidewall to find the correct tire size (e.g., 10×2 or 8.5×2). Order a replacement tube (and tire if worn) that matches exactly — using the wrong size will cause problems. Inner tubes typically cost $5–15 and tires cost $15–35 depending on size and brand.

Tools and Parts You Need

  • Plastic tire levers (2–3) — never metal ones
  • Hex key set (usually 4mm, 5mm, and 6mm)
  • Adjustable wrench or socket set (for axle nuts)
  • Replacement inner tube (same size as original)
  • Tire pump with pressure gauge
  • Rubber gloves (optional but recommended)
  • Torque wrench (optional but ideal for final tightening)
  • Container to store small bolts/nuts

Optional but highly recommended: a small tub of tire mounting paste (or soapy water) makes seating the tire bead much easier. A workstand or table that raises the scooter to a comfortable working height will save your back.

Tube vs Tubeless: Know What You Have

Most scooters under $600 use tube-type tires — these have a separate inner tube inside the tire, just like a bicycle. Tubeless tires (found on premium models like the Ninebot Max series and Xiaomi 4) rely on the rim and tire creating an airtight seal, with sealant fluid inside instead of a tube.

To identify which type you have: deflate the tire completely and try to pull the tire away from the rim at one point. If you see a separate black rubber tube, it's tube-type. If you don't, it's tubeless.

For tubeless tires: Small punctures can often be fixed with a tubeless plug kit ($15) in about 10 minutes without removing the wheel. For large cuts or if the sidewall is damaged, the tire needs replacing. The procedure is similar to tube-type but skips the tube steps.

Step 1: Remove the Wheel

This is the step that varies most between scooter models. Generally:

  1. Turn the scooter off. Fold it down to a stable working position or lay it on its side on a mat.
  2. For front wheels: Use a hex key (usually 4mm–6mm) to remove the axle bolt from both sides. Some models have a quick-release mechanism. The wheel will drop out once both bolts are removed.
  3. For rear wheels: Rear wheels are more complex because the motor hub runs through them. You'll need to disconnect the motor cable (usually a waterproof connector near the deck), then remove the axle nuts from both sides. Take a photo before disassembly — rear wheels have washers and spacers that must go back in the correct order.
  4. Place all bolts, nuts, and washers in a container immediately — they're small and easy to lose.
Motor Wire Warning

On rear wheels, the motor cable runs through the fork. When removing the wheel, don't pull sharply on this cable — strain on the connector can cause intermittent motor faults. Support the wheel as you remove it and ease the cable connector apart gently.

Step 2: Remove the Old Tire and Tube

  1. Fully deflate the tire by pressing the valve pin with a pen or valve cap tool. Wait until all air is out.
  2. Using your thumbs, push both sides of the tire toward the center of the rim. This loosens the tire bead from the rim edge, which is essential for the next step.
  3. Insert a plastic tire lever between the tire bead and the rim edge at the point opposite the valve stem. Hook the lever under the bead and lever it over the rim edge. Use a second lever 3–4 inches away to do the same. Work around the rim until one side of the tire bead is completely over the rim.
  4. Reach inside the tire and pull the inner tube out, starting from the valve stem (push the valve through from the outside first).
  5. Inspect the old tube to find the puncture — inflate it slightly and listen for hissing, or dip it in water to find bubbles. Once found, check the corresponding spot on the tire's inside for the object that caused it (a thorn, glass shard, or wire). Run your finger carefully around the inside of the tire — don't skip this step or the new tube will flat immediately.
  6. If the tire itself is worn, cracked, or damaged, remove it fully by levering the second bead over the rim.

Step 3: Install the New Tube and Tire

  1. If installing a new tire, start by seating one bead fully on the rim by pressing it over the rim edge by hand. Work around the entire circumference — it should snap in place without tools.
  2. Inflate the new inner tube just slightly — enough that it holds a round shape but is still easily deformed. This prevents pinch flats during installation.
  3. Insert the valve stem through the hole in the rim, then tuck the rest of the tube inside the tire, making sure it lies evenly without folds or twists.
  4. Starting at the valve stem, begin pressing the open tire bead over the rim edge using your thumbs. Work in both directions from the valve stem simultaneously, meeting at the bottom.
  5. The last section (directly opposite the valve stem) is the hardest. Use a tire lever only if absolutely necessary, and work carefully to avoid pinching the tube. If using a lever, position it as close to the rim edge as possible and check the tube isn't caught before levering.
  6. Once the tire is seated, press the valve stem inward to make sure the tube isn't caught between the tire bead and rim near the valve.
  7. Inflate to the correct pressure (printed on the tire sidewall — usually 40–65 psi for e-scooters) and check that the bead is seated evenly all the way around.

Step 4: Reinstall the Wheel

  1. For rear wheels, reconnect the motor cable connector — ensure it clicks fully into place and the waterproofing collar is properly sealed.
  2. Slide the axle through the fork, replacing washers and spacers in the exact order you photographed before removal.
  3. Hand-tighten both axle nuts or bolts, then tighten to spec with a wrench. For most scooters, the torque spec is 15–20 Nm — firm but not over-tightened. An over-tightened axle can crack aluminum forks.
  4. Check wheel alignment — the tire should run centered in the fork with equal clearance on both sides.
  5. Spin the wheel by hand and check for any wobble or rubbing against the brake disc or brake pads.
Pro Tip

After reinstalling the wheel, take the scooter for a slow test ride in a safe area before heading onto roads. Check that the wheel spins freely, brakes engage properly, and there's no vibration that wasn't there before.

How to Avoid Pinch Flats

A pinch flat (also called a "snake bite") happens when the tube gets pinched between the tire bead and the rim during installation, creating two parallel punctures. They're frustrating because the new tube goes flat almost immediately after the repair. Here's how to avoid them:

  • Always inflate the tube slightly before installation — a completely flat tube is much more likely to get pinched.
  • Work systematically around the rim from the valve stem outward — don't try to rush the last section in.
  • Before fully inflating, push the valve stem inward and squeeze the tire all around the rim to check for pinched tube material.
  • Inflate in stages — pump to 20 psi, check the bead is seated evenly, then inflate to full pressure.
  • Never use metal tools to lever the bead into place — this is the single biggest cause of pinch flats.

Most e-scooter tires last 2,000–3,000 miles under normal use. Replace them when the tread depth reaches 1.5mm (many tires have wear indicator grooves that disappear at this point), when you notice a significant increase in flats, or when the sidewalls show cracks or bulges. Rear tires wear faster than fronts due to motor traction — check the rear more frequently.

For rougher surfaces or for heavier riders, the higher pressure also provides better puncture resistance. Check pressure weekly — e-scooter tires lose 2–5 psi per week naturally.
