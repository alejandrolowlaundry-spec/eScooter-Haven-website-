Ninebot Max Tire Replacement Guide: DIY Steps & Tips

March 2025 8 min read Written by Alex Rivera

Key Takeaways

  • Gather all necessary tools (including tire levers and a torque wrench) before starting to avoid frustration.
  • Re-installing the tire bead is the most challenging step; using soapy water and a ratchet strap is a proven trick.
  • Always torque the axle nut to the manufacturer's specification (approx. 40 Nm) for critical safety.
  • Consider upgrading to aftermarket tires for potentially better puncture resistance or ride quality.

Your Ninebot Max is a workhorse of reliability, but even the toughest tires eventually wear down. Whether you're facing a flat that won't seal or your tread is looking dangerously smooth, knowing how to replace your scooter's tires is an essential skill for any rider. It saves you time and money, and gets you back on the road faster than waiting for a repair shop.

While the task might seem daunting at first glance, especially with the Max's renowned tubeless, self-healing tires, it's a manageable DIY project with the right tools and guidance. This comprehensive guide will walk you through the entire process, from diagnosing when you need a change to the final torque check, ensuring your scooter rides like new with confidence and safety.

When to Replace Your Ninebot Max Tires

Knowing when to swap your tires is the first step. The Ninebot Max G30 series comes with 10-inch tubeless, self-sealing tires that are incredibly durable, but they aren't immortal. Look for these key signs of wear: visibly low tread depth, especially in the center; multiple punctures or gashes that the sealant can't handle; sidewall cracks or damage; and consistent air pressure loss. If the scooter feels less stable or you notice increased slipping, particularly on wet surfaces, your tread is likely compromised.

Regular inspection is crucial. We recommend a visual check every few weeks. The original tires can last anywhere from 1,000 to 3,000 miles depending on your weight, riding surface, and braking habits. Aggressive braking accelerates wear. Don't wait until the tire is completely bald or you have a catastrophic blowout. Proactive replacement ensures you're never stranded and maintains optimal performance and safety for every ride.

Tools & Parts You'll Need

Failing to prepare is preparing for a frustrating afternoon. You cannot do this job with just a basic multi-tool. Here is the essential toolkit: a set of hex keys (Allen wrenches) – primarily 4mm, 5mm, and 6mm; a 15mm socket or wrench for the front axle nut; a torque wrench (non-negotiable for safety); plastic tire levers (metal can damage the rim); a valve core tool; a pump that can reach 45-50 PSI; and dish soap mixed with water in a spray bottle.

For parts, you'll need the new tire itself. Ensure it's specifically for the Ninebot Max G30 (10x2.5 inches). You can choose another OEM self-healing tire or explore aftermarket options like solid/honeycomb tires (for zero flats but a harsher ride) or different rubber compounds. You will also need fresh sealant (about 60ml per tire) if your new tire isn't pre-filled. Having a ratchet strap on hand is a highly recommended trick for seating the stubborn bead.

Step-by-Step Removal of the Old Tire

Step 1: Preparation. Fully deflate the old tire using the valve core tool. Place your scooter securely on a stable surface. For the rear tire, you'll need to remove the motor assembly. Start by disconnecting the motor phase wire connector (usually under a plastic cover near the deck) and the brake disc sensor wire.

Step 2: Wheel Removal. For the front wheel, simply loosen and remove the 15mm axle nut and washer, then slide the wheel out. For the rear, after disconnecting wires, remove the axle nuts on both sides. The motor/wheel assembly will drop out. Be careful with the brake disc and wiring.

Step 3: Breaking the Bead. This is the hardest physical part. The tire bead is sealed tightly to the rim. Use your tire levers to pry one small section of the tire bead up and over the edge of the rim. Once you have a section started, work your way around the wheel, prying the bead off completely on one side. Then, you can pull the tire off the wheel entirely. Take your time to avoid damaging the rim.

Installing & Seating the New Tire

Step 1: Initial Mounting. Start by adding sealant if needed. Pour about 60ml directly into the new tire before mounting it. Then, place one side of the new tire's bead onto the rim. This can usually be done by hand. Work your way around until one side is fully seated in the rim's center channel.

Step 2: The Second Bead. The final bead is notoriously difficult. Ensure the first bead stays in the deep center channel of the rim across from where you're working—this gives you slack. Use tire levers to hook the second bead over the rim edge, working in small increments. Pro Tip: Liberally spray the bead and rim with your soapy water solution. This acts as a lubricant.

Step 3: Seating the Bead. Once the tire is physically on the rim, the bead must 'snap' into place against the rim walls to hold air. Inflate the tire to the maximum PSI rating on the sidewall (often 55 PSI). You should hear loud pops and snaps. If the bead won't seat, a ratchet strap wrapped around the tire's circumference can compress it and force the beads outward against the rim. This trick solves 90% of seating issues.

Reassembly, Final Checks & Tips

Once the tire is holding air, deflate it to your normal riding pressure (around 40-45 PSI for best comfort and grip). Reinstall the wheel. For the rear motor, carefully guide the axle back into the swingarms, reconnect the motor and sensor wires, and replace the washers and nuts. For the front, simply slide it back in. This is the most critical safety step: use your torque wrench to tighten the axle nuts to the manufacturer's specification, which is approximately 40 Newton-meters (Nm). Do not guess this.

Spin the wheel to ensure it rotates freely without rubbing on the brake caliper or fender. Test the brakes before riding. Take a short, cautious test ride in a safe area to listen for any unusual noises. Finally, check the air pressure again after 24 hours. Remember to periodically check the axle nut torque for the first few rides. Congratulations—you've just mastered one of the most valuable maintenance tasks for your Ninebot Max, ensuring many more miles of reliable riding.
