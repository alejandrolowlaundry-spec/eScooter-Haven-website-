Michigan Electric Scooter Laws: A 2024 Rider's Guide

March 2025 6 min read Written by Alex Rivera

Key Takeaways

  • Michigan law defines e-scooters as "low-speed" vehicles with specific speed and power limits.
  • Riders must be at least 16 years old and are encouraged, but not always required, to wear a helmet.
  • E-scooters are generally prohibited on sidewalks and must ride on the road or in bike lanes.
  • Local city rules in places like Detroit and Ann Arbor can override or add to state laws.

Thinking of zipping through the streets of Detroit, cruising along the Grand River, or exploring Ann Arbor on an electric scooter? Michigan offers fantastic urban and scenic routes for e-scooter enthusiasts. However, before you hit the throttle, it's crucial to understand the legal landscape. Unlike cars or bicycles, e-scooters occupy a unique space in transportation law, and knowing the rules is key to a safe, fun, and ticket-free ride.

This comprehensive guide breaks down everything you need to know about electric scooter laws in the Great Lakes State. We'll cover state-wide regulations established by Public Act 107 of 2020, dive into essential safety rules, and highlight how local city ordinances can affect your ride. Whether you own a scooter or use a shared rental service, this article will equip you with the knowledge to ride legally and confidently across Michigan.

Michigan's Legal Definition of an Electric Scooter

Under Michigan law, specifically Public Act 107 of 2020, an electric scooter is legally defined as a "low-speed" vehicle. This definition is critical because it determines where you can ride and what rules apply. To be considered a legal e-scooter in Michigan, the device must have a floorboard for standing, handlebars, and an electric motor. It cannot be powered by a combustion engine.

The law sets clear limits on performance: the scooter's maximum speed on a paved level surface cannot exceed 25 miles per hour. Furthermore, the motor must be incapable of propelling the device faster than 25 mph without human power (like kicking). There is no explicit horsepower limit in the statute, but the speed cap effectively governs the motor's power. This definition separates e-scooters from mopeds, motorcycles, and other motor vehicles, placing them in their own category for regulation. Understanding this is the first step to ensuring your scooter is street-legal in the state.

Where You Can and Cannot Ride

Location is one of the most important and often misunderstood aspects of e-scooter law. Michigan state law provides a general framework, but local rules are paramount. At the state level, electric scooters are generally prohibited from being operated on sidewalks. The intent of the law is to have them function as a roadway vehicle, similar to bicycles.

So, where should you ride? The ideal place is in a designated bicycle lane. If a bike lane is available, you must use it. If no bicycle lane is present, you must operate your e-scooter on the far right side of the roadway, traveling in the same direction as traffic. You may also use shoulder or curb lanes. Riding against traffic is illegal and extremely dangerous. Some cities may have specific, signed paths or greenways where scooters are permitted. Always defer to posted local signage, as a city ordinance can further restrict or permit riding in certain areas, like downtown pedestrian zones or parks.

Age, Helmet, and License Requirements

Michigan sets clear baseline requirements for who can ride an e-scooter. You must be at least 16 years old to operate an electric scooter on a public street or highway. There is no requirement for a driver's license, vehicle registration, or insurance at the state level, which makes e-scooters accessible to many residents and visitors.

Helmet laws are nuanced. State law does not mandate helmet use for riders 16 and older. However, it strongly encourages it as a critical safety practice. For riders under the age of 19, the law requires that they wear a helmet that is properly fitted and fastened. This helmet must meet the standards set by the U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT). Regardless of the law, wearing a helmet is the single most effective way to prevent serious head injury in a fall or collision. We strongly recommend every rider, regardless of age, make a helmet a non-negotiable part of their gear.

Key Safety and Operational Rules

Beyond where and who can ride, Michigan law outlines specific operational rules for safe riding. First, you cannot carry passengers on an e-scooter designed for a single rider. The device is meant for one person standing on the floorboard. Second, you must keep at least one hand on the handlebars at all times while in motion.

When it comes to rights and responsibilities, e-scooter riders have all the rights and duties applicable to a driver of a vehicle under Michigan's Vehicle Code, except where specifically stated otherwise. This means you must obey all traffic signals, signs, and right-of-way rules. You must signal your turns, yield to pedestrians, and not ride in a careless or reckless manner. Importantly, Operating While Intoxicated (OWI) laws apply to e-scooter riders. You can be arrested for drunk or drugged driving on a scooter, facing the same severe penalties as you would in a car.

Local Ordinances: Detroit, Ann Arbor & Beyond

State law provides the foundation, but Michigan cities have the authority to enact stricter local ordinances. This is the most crucial layer for riders to research. For example, the City of Detroit has its own detailed ordinance governing shared and privately-owned scooters. It may restrict riding in certain business districts, set specific parking rules to avoid sidewalk clutter, and impose fines for violations.

Ann Arbor, Grand Rapids, and Lansing also have their own regulations, often focused on the management of shared scooter fleets but affecting all riders. These can include geofenced "no-ride" or "slow-speed" zones, mandatory parking corrals, and specific rules for riding on campus or in large parks. Before you ride in a new city, a quick online search for "[City Name] electric scooter ordinance" or checking the local government's transportation page can save you from unexpected tickets. Always remember: when a local law conflicts with state law, the stricter rule applies.
