What is a Bri Flip? BMX Trick Guide for Scooter Riders

March 2025 7 min read Written by Alex Rivera

Key Takeaways

  • The Bri Flip is an advanced BMX trick combining a backflip and a 360 spin.
  • Mastering foundational tricks like 180s, 360s, and kickouts is essential first.
  • Electric scooters require specific modifications for high-level aerial tricks.
  • Progression, safety gear, and spotter help are non-negotiable for advanced riding.

If you've been deep in the world of action sports videos or hanging around the skate park, you might have heard the term "Bri Flip" thrown around. Originating from BMX, the Bri Flip is a complex, stylish aerial maneuver that combines a backflip with a 360-degree spin. It's a trick that demands serious air awareness, commitment, and skill. For electric scooter riders who have mastered the basics and are looking to push their freestyle limits, understanding tricks like the Bri Flip represents the pinnacle of progression.

While attempting a full Bri Flip on an electric scooter is an elite-level feat reserved for very few professionals, breaking down its components offers invaluable lessons for all riders. This post isn't a guide to hucking your first Bri Flip this weekend. Instead, we'll explore what this trick is, the fundamental skills it builds upon, and how the pursuit of such advanced techniques can make you a safer, more controlled, and more confident rider overall. Think of it as a masterclass in aerial control and progression planning.

Deconstructing the Bri Flip: A BMX Legacy

The Bri Flip, named after BMX rider Brian Foster, is a trick that has captivated audiences for years. In its pure form, the rider launches from a ramp, initiates a backflip, and during the rotation, adds a full 360-degree horizontal spin. The result is a breathtaking corkscrew motion. It's a trick that requires not just the physical ability to generate height and rotation, but an almost intuitive sense of where you are in the air at all times—a skill known as "air awareness."

For the electric scooter community, such BMX tricks serve as both inspiration and a technical blueprint. While our machines are heavier and have a different center of gravity, the physics of rotation and the mental approach are directly transferable. Studying these tricks highlights the importance of commitment. Half-hearted attempts at advanced rotations often lead to crashes. Understanding the Bri Flip's complexity reinforces a core riding principle: you must build up to major tricks in a systematic, step-by-step manner, respecting both your skill level and your equipment's limits.

Essential Foundation Tricks for Scooter Riders

Before the word "flip" even enters your vocabulary, your trick book needs to be solid. The path to advanced aerials is paved with perfected basics. These are the non-negotiable building blocks every aspiring freestyle scooter rider must master in a safe, foam pit or on a gentle grass slope before ever trying them on concrete.

  1. Bunny Hops & Manuals: Absolute control over your scooter's takeoff and balance is step one. A powerful, controlled bunny hop is the engine for every aerial trick.
  2. 180s and 360s: You must be completely comfortable spinning your scooter horizontally. Practice both frontside and backside 180s until they are second nature, then progress to 360s. This builds the spin technique needed for part of a Bri Flip.
  3. Tailwhips & Kickouts: Tricks that involve moving the deck independently from your body drastically improve your board control and air awareness. A simple kickout (turning the bars mid-air) is a great start.
  4. Backflip Progression: This is a massive step. Start into a foam pit or on a trampoline with a modified scooter. Use a resi ramp or airbag. The feeling of rotating backwards must become familiar before adding any other axis of spin.

Is Your Electric Scooter Built for This?

Your average commuter electric scooter is not built for the forces of advanced freestyle riding. Attempting high-impact tricks on one can lead to catastrophic failure of the stem, fork, or deck, resulting in serious injury. If you're serious about progression into aerials and spins, you need to consider your hardware.

Freestyle-specific electric scooters exist and are built with reinforced components, such as: thick, heat-treated steel or alloy decks; heavy-duty, compression-based steering systems (IHC or SCS); and reinforced forks and wheels. These scooters prioritize strength over lightweight portability. Additionally, for practicing flips, many pros use a dedicated "practice deck"—a stripped-down scooter without electronics or a battery to save weight and cost during crashes in foam pits. The key takeaway is: know your scooter's limits. Pushing the envelope requires equipment designed to handle it.

Safety and Progression: The Non-Negotiables

Chasing tricks like the Bri Flip isn't just about courage; it's about intelligent, safe practice. Rushing the process is the fastest way to a long-term injury. Here’s your safety and progression framework.

First, gear up like your life depends on it—because it does. A full-face helmet (MTB/Downhill certified), knee and elbow pads, wrist guards, and a chest protector or padded jacket are essential for high-impact practice. Second, use the right training tools. Foam pits, resi (soft foam) ramps, and airbags are not for beginners only; they are the tools professionals use to learn new tricks safely. Third, get a spotter/coach. Having an experienced rider guide you, provide feedback, and even physically assist in the learning phase (e.g., spotting a backflip) is invaluable. Finally, film your attempts. Reviewing footage helps you diagnose issues with your takeoff, tuck, and landing that you can't feel in the moment.

Mindset and Respect in the Scooter Community

The pursuit of advanced tricks fosters more than just physical skill; it builds a mindset of patience, resilience, and respect. You will fall. You will fail. You will have setbacks. Learning to analyze a crash, get back up, and try again with a adjusted approach is a life lesson born at the skatepark. This journey teaches you to respect the trick, the park, and other riders.

Furthermore, understanding the history and difficulty of tricks like the Bri Flip cultivates respect for all action sports athletes. It bridges communities—BMX, scooter, skateboard. Always be mindful of park etiquette: wait your turn, don't snake other riders' lines, and offer encouragement. Your progression is your own journey; don't compare your chapter 3 to someone else's chapter 20. Celebrate small wins, like finally landing a clean 360 or getting comfortable with the feeling of being upside down in a foam pit. These are the real victories on the path to mastery.
